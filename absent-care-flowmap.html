Absent Care, Extracted Hope

Mapping Reproductive Injustice in Rural West Bengal

India's fertility industry was built on patients crossing borders, travelling from wealthier countries to Indian clinics for cheaper treatment. Reproductive tourism scholarship has documented this flow extensively. But as competition among Kolkata's 150-plus private Assisted Reproductive Technology (ART) clinics intensifies, the industry turns inward, toward its own rural hinterland. Doctors travel from Kolkata to semi-urban towns, form alliances with local practitioners, and screen women from surrounding villages. These women become the new patient base. The profit no longer flows only from abroad. It is drawn from communities where public fertility care has never existed.

This map traces that extraction. It follows women as they move from villages with no qualified doctors to semi-urban clinics with no fertility infrastructure to a city where treatment is concentrated but emergency care is absent from the clinics that provide it. It documents what is available at each level, what is missing, and how far women must travel to compensate for that absence. ART demands repeated monitoring, hormonal regulation, proximity to emergency intervention. The women this industry recruits have none of it. They travel, return, travel again, and bear the medical risks of treatment in communities where those risks cannot be managed.

Built from ethnographic fieldwork across rural and semi-urban West Bengal between 2024 and 2025, this map locates infertility cases, traces referral pathways, and measures the distances women cover in pursuit of a diagnosis and a chance at conception. These journeys do not appear in hospital registers, insurance records, or government health surveys. This map is an attempt to make them visible, and to document a structure of care that operates through the very inequalities it claims to address.

Village Level

In most rural villages of West Bengal, there are no qualified doctors. The nearest government facility is a sub-centre, typically staffed by a single Auxiliary Nurse Midwife who provides immunisation, antenatal check-ups, and contraception. None of these services address infertility.

So the women who cannot conceive turn to what is available. Unqualified practitioners locally known as "village doctors," former assistants or compounders who once worked under qualified doctors and have since opened their own small clinics. They charge as little as 50 rupees, less than a dollar, per visit. Women also seek help from homeopathy practitioners, Ayurvedic healers, and faith healers at dargahs and temples. These are not choices made out of preference. They are the only options that exist. The diagnostic tools to identify conditions such as tubal blockage, hormonal imbalance, or male factor infertility are entirely absent at the village level. Without them, women go undiagnosed for years, moving from one provider to the next, spending what little they have, often blamed for a condition no one around them has the means to name.

Semi-Urban Level

Women seeking a diagnosis must travel to the nearest semi-urban town, typically 10 to 60 km from their villages. Gynaecologists, general practitioners, and registered medical practitioners at this level operate small private clinics with limited diagnostic capacity, offering basic ultrasound and pathology services. Government hospitals in many of these semi-urban towns maintain gynaecology departments, but the specialists meant to staff them are largely absent. A national capacity assessment found that only 10% of medical officers at sub-district facilities provided any infertility treatment, while 73% referred patients elsewhere (Chauhan et al., 2018). Advanced diagnostics required for infertility evaluation, such as AMH testing, hysterosalpingography, and hormonal panels, also remain unavailable in many of these semi-urban towns. Fertility treatment and ART laboratories are similarly lacking across most facilities at this level.

This infrastructural absence shapes what follows. As competition among Kolkata's private ART clinics intensifies, they look outward for patients. Clinics have established financial arrangements with semi-urban practitioners, who direct women into urban treatment circuits in exchange for referral fees. When local practitioners identify infertility, they do not initiate treatment. Instead, patients are held until ART clinicians from Kolkata conduct periodic screening camps at these clinics, typically twice a month. Those selected are referred to the city for treatment. The local practitioner profits from the referral, the urban clinic secures a steady patient base, and the woman enters a treatment pathway that requires repeated travel to a city where she has no social or material support.

Urban Level: Kolkata

Kolkata hosts over 150 private ART clinics competing for a limited urban patient base. This saturation drives the industry outward into rural and semi-urban West Bengal, where an untapped population of infertile women has never had access to fertility care. The referral arrangements with semi-urban practitioners and the periodic screening camps are the commercial strategy through which urban clinics secure patients the city alone can no longer provide.

Urban patients access this infrastructure as consumers with choices. They compare clinics, seek second opinions, switch providers, and travel to other cities for specialised treatment. Rural women enter the same system under entirely different conditions. They arrive through a single referral chain they did not choose. They cannot compare clinics or seek alternatives. They travel 100 to 350 km for each visit.

The system these women enter is not structured to care for them. It is structured to extract from them. A single IVF cycle costs $1,200 to $3,000. For a household earning $96 to $120 per month, this represents 10 to 22 months of total income. Most families can barely afford one cycle. They exhaust their savings, borrow from relatives, sell land, or take high-interest informal loans. Neither Ayushman Bharat nor West Bengal's Swasthya Sathi scheme covers fertility treatment. Banks do not lend for it. The investment is enormous. The odds are against them. IVF succeeds roughly 40% of the time. Most cycles fail.

The clinical structure compounds this harm. ART clinics operate as outpatient facilities, without emergency wards, inpatient beds, or overnight monitoring. Women are sent home the same day after procedures. For rural patients, home is hours away. They administer hormonal injections alone. When something goes wrong, there is nowhere close enough to go. An untreated complication can end not only a cycle but a woman's ability to conceive again, turning a family's financial sacrifice into a permanent loss. The system recruits these women, charges them what they cannot afford, gives them odds that require multiple attempts, and operates without the infrastructure to keep them safe through even one.

India's ART Act, enacted in 2022, was meant to regulate the fertility industry after two decades without oversight. It mandates clinic registration, classifies facilities into two levels, establishes a national registry, and penalises sex selection and gamete trafficking. But the Act regulates clinics. It does not address patients.

The law contains no provisions for geographic equity, no mandate for public-sector ART expansion, no regulation of treatment costs, and no requirement for emergency care infrastructure at fertility clinics. It does not regulate the financial arrangements between urban ART clinics and semi-urban practitioners who direct patients into treatment circuits. It does not address the outreach camps through which Kolkata's private clinics recruit patients from communities that lack basic diagnostic facilities. Ayushman Bharat and West Bengal's Swasthya Sathi both exclude fertility treatment. Banks do not lend for it. At the time of the Act's passage, the Parliamentary Standing Committee noted that only six public-sector IVF centres existed across the entire country (Kashyap and Tripathi, 2023).

Every stage of the journey this map documents falls outside the Act's scope. The rural woman who goes undiagnosed for years because village-level healthcare cannot identify her condition. The semi-urban practitioner who holds her for a visiting ART team rather than treating her locally. The urban clinic that charges her a year's household income for a procedure with a 40% success rate, then sends her home the same day without emergency support. The complication that goes untreated because the nearest hospital is hours away. The family that sells its land to fund a second cycle that may never happen. The Act governs the clinic at the centre of this pipeline. It says nothing about the women who pass through it.

Through this map, I have tried to make visible what policy does not see. The ART Act regulates what happens inside the clinic. This map documents what happens outside it: the distances women travel, the infrastructure that does not exist along the way, the financial ruin that follows a failed cycle, and the emergency care that is absent when complications arise.

It traces a system that recruits rural women into commercial fertility treatment without building the conditions under which that treatment can be safe, affordable, or accessible.

This map is not a policy document. It is an ethnographic record of what the current framework leaves out, and an argument that any regulation of India's fertility industry that does not account for the patient's full journey, from village to clinic and back, fails the very people it should protect.
