About This Archive

Documenting Reproductive Injustice, Amplifying Marginalized Voices

This project creates an interactive digital archive documenting the lived experiences of infertile Muslim women in West Bengal, India, grounded in ethnographic research conducted across urban and rural sites. As a marginalized religious minority in a patriarchal, pronatalist society, infertile Muslim women experience unique forms of reproductive suffering that are both social and biological. In contexts where womanhood is defined through motherhood, childlessness leads to stigma, isolation, and gender-based violence. For Muslim women, these struggles are compounded by Islamophobia and state neglect, which sustain economic and political marginalization and limit access to equitable reproductive care.

The archive makes visible that infertility among Muslim women is not an individual problem but a consequence of structural discrimination. By tracing how poverty, segregation, environmental harm, and healthcare neglect shape reproductive outcomes, it exposes state-produced reproductive injustice and counters narratives that blame women while hiding the structural roots of infertility.

Mapping Geographic Exploitation

Visualizing Networks of Forced Dependency

Through interactive Leaflet mapping, the project visualizes networks forcing rural Muslim women to travel hundreds of kilometers to Kolkata's fertility hub, making visible geographic distances, infrastructural disparities, and economic extraction created by decades of uneven state investment.

Preserving Pluralistic Care

Traditional Healing & Alternative Pathways

The archive preserves pluralistic care pathways, including traditional healing practices, religious consultation, homeopathy, and Ayurveda — practices rooted in relational moral worlds and embodied knowledge systems that are rapidly vanishing under globalization and the expansion of Assisted Reproductive Technologies.

Women's Reproductive Experiences

Centering Lived Realities of Infertility

By centering the voices of infertile Muslim women, the archive documents how womanhood defined through motherhood leads to stigma, isolation, and violence — and how these struggles are compounded by Islamophobia, state neglect, and the erasure of marginalized ways of knowing and healing.

From the Field

Ethnographic Research in West Bengal

Fieldwork conducted across urban and rural sites, centering the voices and experiences of women navigating reproductive health in their communities.

By integrating national health data with ethnographic accounts and interactive mapping, the archive serves as both a scholarly resource across humanities, social sciences, and public health, and as a powerful advocacy tool. Once established within academic and practitioner communities, the project will expand to include a decolonial community platform. The goal is to invite diverse contributions in the form of poems, stories, arts, artifacts, photographs, blogs, and essays from scholars and practitioners, particularly from the Global South.

The archive will ensure knowledge remains relevant and responsive to the communities it represents. It will continually evolve through community feedback, updated contributions, and ongoing field engagement.

A Personal Note

"This project is deeply personal to me. As an Indian Muslim woman, I have seen firsthand how inequality and discrimination determine who receives care and who is left behind. I grew up watching women in my community navigate not only illness but also the social and moral judgments attached to it. Creating this archive is my way of honoring their experiences and ensuring they are not erased. By sharing these narratives, I hope to open up new conversations about inclusive reproductive health in India."

— Gungun Islam

About the Researcher

Gungun Islam

Anthropologist at Michigan State University specializing in medical anthropology. Her research sits at the intersection of reproduction, religion, gender, and structural inequality in South Asia.

Her work examines how postcolonial governance, Islamophobia, and patriarchal structures produce reproductive injustice for marginalized Muslim women in India, drawing on ethnographic fieldwork, feminist theory, and digital humanities methodologies.

Call for Contributions

This archive is a living project. We invite researchers, community members, and storytellers to contribute their voices and expand this collective record.

✦

What You Can Share

We welcome a range of contributions that center women's reproductive experiences and knowledge systems.

  • Personal stories and oral histories
  • Poetry, essays, and creative writing
  • Photographs and visual documentation
  • Scholarly articles and research notes
  • Audio recordings and interviews
  • Traditional healing knowledge (with consent)
↗

How to Get Involved

All contributions follow a careful ethical protocol to ensure dignity, consent, and community ownership of shared narratives.

  • Review our submission guidelines
  • Contact the research team for guidance
  • Informed consent process for all contributors
  • IRB-approved ethical protocols
  • Anonymous contributions accepted
  • Community review of published materials
