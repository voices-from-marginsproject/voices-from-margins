Navigating Reproductive Health at the Margins

An ethnographic essay exploring reproductive precarity, infrastructural neglect, and women's endurance on the margins of West Bengal.

By Gungun Islam  ·  28th April, 2026

I first met Saira in the waiting room of a private fertility clinic in Kolkata, on a sweltering afternoon in May 2024. I was there for my preliminary summer fieldwork. The room was crowded and airless, thick with heat and the low hum of people waiting. We stood side by side near a small water tap, waiting for our turn. As we spoke about the heat, it became clear that for Saira, the exhaustion had not begun in the clinic. She had been travelling since early morning from her village in North 24 Parganas. There are no assisted reproductive technology clinics in rural North 24 Parganas; most people seeking fertility treatment travel to Kolkata, crossing long distances to reach the nearest provider (to understand the distances rural individuals must travel to access care, see the interactive map on the following page). Reaching the clinic had taken her nearly three hours, changing buses and trains and standing the entire way. By the time she arrived, her clothes were damp with sweat, her body already worn down.

Saira was thirty-four years old, married at seventeen, and in all the years since had never conceived. When I first met her, she was in the middle of her first cycle of IVF, a process that requires repeated visits for injections, scans, and consultations. Each one demanded that she undertake the same long journey again. For the past two months, she had been making these trips alone, moving back and forth between a village where treatment was unavailable and a city where it could be accessed. The treatment had already begun to take a visible toll on her body, as she described how the injections caused her whole body to swell, leaving her veins feeling hard and heavy, while the pain in her lower back, on some days, made it difficult for her even to stand. Travelling in this condition puts immense pressure on her body. But the distance she travelled to access care was only one part of what she endured. At the clinic, the doctors traced the source of her condition back to the place she had come from. Years of bathing in pond water had led to untreated pelvic inflammatory disease. Over time, this had scarred and blocked both of her fallopian tubes. By the time it was diagnosed, the damage was irreversible, leaving IVF as the only option.

But bathing in a pond was never a choice. Saira's village sits in North 24 Parganas, one of the worst arsenic-affected districts in West Bengal, where groundwater itself is unsafe, contaminated above WHO limits (Chakraborti et al. 2009). In trying to avoid this contaminated groundwater, the community is pushed toward surface water, relying on the village pond. However, what appears as a choice of shift between sources is, in practice, a movement between two forms of harm, both shaped by the state failure to provide safe water infrastructure.

Arsenic contamination across the Ganga-Brahmaputra-Meghna floodplains has been known for decades, leaching naturally from alluvial sediments into shallow aquifers. First identified in the 1970s and recognized as a major public health crisis by the late 1990s (Chakraborti et al. 2010), it has been met with a state response marked by delay and underinvestment. Deep tube wells that access safer aquifers remain too expensive for most rural households and have never been installed at the scale required (Sengupta and Mandal 2010). National and State Programs such as Jal Jeevan Mission and Jal Swapno have promised piped water access to all arsenic affected regions but implementation has repeatedly bypassed rural settlements like Saira's.

In practice, this leaves villagers with no safe alternative. In Saira's village, the entire community depends on a single government-installed deep tube well, far too few for its population. Wealthier households install private wells at significant cost, but for families like Saira's, this is not an option. Women therefore walk long distances to collect drinking water from the limited safe sources available, while for everything else bathing, washing, cleaning they rely on the village pond. These ponds are shared with livestock, used for jute retting, and receive runoff, including untreated brick-kiln effluent. Over time, this produces continuous exposure to contaminated water through everyday bodily practices.

Groundwater Arsenic Across West Bengal's Districts

Between 1988 and 2008, researchers at the School of Environmental Studies, Jadavpur University, analyzed 140,150 hand tube well water samples across all 19 districts of West Bengal. Their findings revealed that 48.1% of tube wells contained arsenic above the WHO guideline of 10 µg/L and 23.8% exceeded 50 µg/L. Nine districts were classified as highly affected, five as mildly affected, and five as unaffected (Chakraborti et al., 2009).

What Water Does to the Womb

What this daily contact with contaminated water means for the reproductive body is only now beginning to be understood. Women in villages like Saira's are exposed to two distinct threats from two different water sources. The groundwater they drink carries arsenic. The pond water they bathe in, wash with, and use for intimate hygiene carries faecal bacteria, agricultural waste, and industrial effluent. Both damage fertility, but through different biological pathways.

Research has shown that chronic arsenic exposure through groundwater disrupts reproductive hormones and damages ovarian follicles. It has been linked to miscarriage, stillbirth, and reduced fertility (Milton et al. 2017, Quansah et al. 2015). A population-level study across 599 Indian districts found that higher groundwater arsenic was significantly associated with increased rates of stillbirth, recurrent pregnancy loss, and infertility (Pinchoff et al. 2022). Several case-control studies have suggested that arsenic plays a role in impairing fertility through oxidative stress and decreased sperm quality (Shen et al. 2013, Susko et al. 2017, Wang et al. 2016). Despite this growing evidence, the precise mechanisms remain poorly understood and largely absent from policy and public health research.

What has received even less attention is how contaminated surface water damages women's fertility. Recent research has begun to trace a biological pathway from contaminated water to infertility that runs through the reproductive body. Czapar et al. (2024), in a study of 436 adolescent girls in western Kenya, found that girls who used pond water for washing were significantly more likely to lose the protective bacteria in their vaginal tract, leaving them vulnerable to infection, compared to those with access to piped water. In another study of women in the United States and Kenya, Sabo et al. (2019) found that vaginal washing with contaminated water, a daily necessity for women without running water, destroys these protective bacteria and allows harmful organisms to take hold. The scale of this problem is significant: in Odisha, India, Torondel et al. (2018) documented that 62.4% of women with poor water and sanitation access had at least one laboratory-confirmed reproductive tract infection.

Once established, these infections do not remain in the lower body. Bacteria ascend silently through the cervix into the uterus and fallopian tubes, producing pelvic inflammatory disease, often without symptoms severe enough to prompt treatment. Crucially, Haggerty et al. (2016) found that women with PID caused by these bacteria had 8.5 times higher risk of persistent infection in the uterus and nearly four times the risk of subsequent infertility, even when no sexually transmitted infection was present. This matters because approximately half of all PID cases test negative for gonorrhoea and chlamydia (Mitchell and Prabhu 2021). The organisms responsible, E. coli, Bacteroides, Streptococcus, are not sexually transmitted. They are the organisms found in faecally contaminated surface water (Pearce et al. 2025).

Many women in India fall victim to this problem, particularly in arsenic-contaminated regions where the government has failed to provide piped water and the few deep tube wells installed are far too few for the communities they serve. Private wells do exist, but they cost around one lakh rupees, about a thousand dollars, which is simply out of reach for most families. Saira's husband is the sole earner, bringing in about 10,000 rupees a month, roughly 100 dollars. So for them, accessing safe water is practically impossible. Within this context, collecting drinking water becomes a deeply gendered responsibility. It is women who travel long distances each day just to bring back drinking water, while relying on the village pond for bathing and domestic use. These ponds hold everything the village puts into them. Jute stalks are submerged for weeks, their decomposition feeding bacterial growth. Livestock wade through the same water, adding faecal matter. Brick kilns discharge untreated effluent carrying heavy metals and chemical waste. The water is neither treated nor monitored. Women use it daily to wash their bodies, clean their genitals, and manage menstrual hygiene, exposing their reproductive tracts directly to these contaminants.

For Saira, this daily exposure did exactly what the studies warn of. Contaminated pond water introduced enteric bacteria into her vaginal tract, disrupting the protective microbiome. Over time, those bacteria ascended silently through her cervix into her uterus and fallopian tubes, producing pelvic inflammatory disease. She had experienced pelvic pain for years but had grown accustomed to it, never recognising it as a symptom of an infection that was progressively destroying her fallopian tubes. By the time anyone looked closely enough to find the cause, there was nothing left to save. Her only option now was IVF.

The doctors instructed her to stop using the village pond and to avoid heavy physical exertion during treatment. These instructions assumed an infrastructure that does not exist. Saira now purchases drinking water at twenty rupees per container, an additional daily expense on a household already borrowing to fund IVF. For bathing, she walks to a more distant and less frequented pond, the only alternative available. Most women in the village avoid making this trip because they must walk back drenched through the village with no place to change, visible to neighbours in wet clothes, a source of shame in a community where women's bodily exposure is policed. The same absence of infrastructure that damaged her body now constrains her capacity to comply with the medical requirements of her own care.

The Politics of the Womb

The structural violence that produces infertility also denies access to its treatment. Saira's village lacks any health facility, trained practitioner, or diagnostic capacity. Infertility is mostly absent from public provision, so women must navigate care on their own. What this means in practice is a three-tier system. At the village level, care is provided by village doctors, unqualified practitioners without training or equipment, charging less than a dollar. At the second level, roughly twenty kilometres away, registered practitioners operate private clinics where they can consult and prescribe, but lack the diagnostic capacity to investigate more complex conditions. At the third level, specialised fertility care exists only in private clinics in urban Kolkata, three hours away, at costs that push families into debt. Each step increases distance, cost, and inaccessibility.

Saira moved through all three tiers over the course of several years. From early in her marriage she experienced chronic pelvic pain, a tearing, needle-like sensation that she lived with constantly. When she reported it, it was dismissed as a normal part of being a woman. Within a patriarchal household, her suffering had no space. Over time, she became used to it and stopped mentioning it. This reflects a broader pattern. In South Asian societies women's pain is often minimised, attributed to emotional causes, and left untreated.

It was only when she could not conceive that anyone took notice and she was sent to the first tier of care, the village doctor, the most accessible option, where she received basic treatment for over a year without any result. She then moved to the second tier, a rural practitioner an hour away, who referred her to a private diagnostic centre in Basirhat for an ultrasound. It identified polycystic ovaries. The doctor began hormonal treatment on this basis alone. Despite Saira's long history of pelvic pain, no one investigated it further. The same medication was prescribed for two years without reassessment or referral, costing 1,500 rupees a month, roughly fifteen dollars. Her husband was never asked to undergo even basic testing. Eventually the cost became unsustainable and Saira was forced to stop.

For three years after that, she received no treatment at all. Then a private ART clinic from Kolkata held an outreach camp in her area. These camps have become common as urban fertility clinics compete for patients from communities with no access to specialist services. For many women, they are the first point of contact with advanced reproductive care. At the camp, a doctor reviewed Saira's history and, for the first time, asked that her husband also be tested. She was referred to the Kolkata clinic. There, she underwent hysterosalpingography, a procedure that had never been available to her before. It confirmed what years of pelvic pain had been signalling all along: both of her fallopian tubes were blocked from long-term infection. An ultrasound also confirmed PCOS, the same condition that had been treated for two years at the second tier. That treatment may have supported ovulation, but conception was impossible because her tubes were blocked. The condition that actually prevented her from conceiving had gone undiagnosed for years because the test required to identify it did not exist at the levels of care she could access. In rural areas, practitioners treat what they can see with the resources available to them. Conditions that need advanced diagnostics remain invisible. The result is years of treatment aimed at one problem while another quietly prevents conception.

However, reaching the third tier of care, the private ART clinic that finally diagnosed Saira correctly, did not resolve the problem. It introduced new ones. The first was economic. Infertility care is almost entirely absent from India's public health programmes, excluded from insurance, and largely unavailable in the public sector (Widge and Cleland 2009, Sharma et al. 2023). Treatment is available only through private clinics at commercial rates. A single IVF cycle in Kolkata costs between 2.5 and 4 lakh rupees, with no guarantee of success. For a household where her husband is the sole earner as a migrant labourer in another city making ten thousand rupees a month, this represents two to three years of total income. To continue care, Saira's family borrowed money and sold assets, tipping the household into debt for a treatment the state's own negligence made necessary.

The second problem was the distance. The treatment itself assumes that the patient lives close to the clinic. Saira does not. She travels six hours round trip for every visit, alone, while managing a hormonal regimen that requires self-administered injections at precise intervals. She described missing injections because the timing fell during travel, or because she could not manage the administration without medical supervision. The clinic that had recruited her from her village through a rural outreach camp, that had invested in identifying her as a patient and drawn her into a treatment cycle costing lakhs of rupees, provided no infrastructure to support her through it. ART clinics in Kolkata do not have emergency services or outpatient services where patients can come in case complications arise. It is more difficult for someone like Saira who is anyway travelling hundreds of kilometres to come to the urban clinic in the first place as there is nothing in her village. The distance the clinic was willing to bridge for the purpose of recruitment, it made no effort to bridge for the purpose of care. There is no satellite facility of the clinic in the village and in case of emergency Saira must travel all the way back to Kolkata.

When I returned to the field and met Saira again in 2025, she told me she had conceived through the IVF cycle. Then she miscarried. When she began bleeding, she called the clinic. It was a Saturday. She was told to come on Monday when she got the appointment. She was alone in her house without her husband, a migrant worker who was in another city gone for work, in a village with no health facility, three hours from the clinic that had told her it could give her a child. She took a photograph of the miscarriage on her phone so she would have something to show the doctor when she could finally get an appointment.

Saira's case forces us to confront the everyday reproductive precarity that women face at the margins of West Bengal. Her infertility was not a random biological event. It was produced over time by a state that failed to provide clean water, failed to build healthcare infrastructure, and then left her to navigate a market-driven fertility industry that recruited her for profit and abandoned her when the treatment failed.

Paul Farmer's concept of structural violence helps us understand what happened to Saira. Farmer argued that poverty and state neglect do not simply create difficult living conditions; they enter the body and produce disease (Farmer 2004). The arsenic in the groundwater, the bacteria in her pond, the silent scarring of her fallopian tubes: these were not misfortunes. They were the biological consequences of political choices about who receives clean water and who does not. Margaret Lock's concept of local biology takes this further, showing that the body is shaped by the specific ecological and political conditions of the place in which a person lives (Lock 1993). Saira's infertility cannot be separated from the arsenic geology of the Gangetic plains, the absence of piped water in her ward, or the five years it took three providers to produce a diagnosis that should have come in the first consultation.

Infertility in contexts like hers is not individual pathology. It is a structural condition. Confronting it demands more than clinical intervention. It requires holding the state accountable for its failure and recognising infertility as a public health consequence of governance neglect, not a private burden to be borne by the poorest households. It also demands accountability from commercial ART clinics that recruit rural women into expensive treatments but vanish when they need care the most.
